# Writing Style Profile: Patrick Tehubijuluw

## Executive Summary

Patrick's writing style is highly approachable, practical, and tutorial-focused with a casual conversational tone. He combines technical expertise with friendly guidance, using short paragraphs, numbered lists, and step-by-step instructions to break down complex concepts into digestible, actionable content. His style is characterized by enthusiasm, encouragement, and a genuine desire to help readers learn and succeed.

---

## Core Characteristics

### Voice & Tone

- **Formality Level**: 3/10
- **Personality**: Friendly, helpful, enthusiastic, slightly playful
- **Emotional Register**: Positive and encouraging, with subtle humor
- **Approachability**: Highly approachable - writes like a knowledgeable colleague sharing tips over coffee

**Key Voice Markers**:
- Uses first person frequently ("I think", "I often", "I would like")
- Direct second-person address ("you will learn", "your assignment", "you could")
- Inclusive "we" when appropriate ("we should", "we can do")
- Encouraging closings: "Have fun!", "See you soon!", "See u next time!", "Happy designing!"
- Occasional emoticons and playful language (";-)", "dream on ;)", "wanna be able to")

### Language Preferences

**Words Used**:
- Simple, everyday vocabulary accessible to all skill levels
- Action verbs (create, add, design, build, help, learn)
- Technical terms with immediate explanations
- Conversational markers ("quite simple", "handy right?", "easy right?")
- Personal pronouns (I, you, we) to create intimacy
- Encouraging adjectives (nice, good, interesting, useful)
- Concrete, specific language (file paths, settings, exact steps)
- Practical references (download links, external resources, examples)

**Words Avoided**:
- Excessive jargon without explanation
- Marketing buzzwords (synergy, leverage, optimize, harness)
- Overly formal or academic language
- Clichés and filler phrases
- Complex sentence structures that obscure meaning
- Passive voice (prefers active, direct statements)
- Negativity or discouragement

**Vocabulary Characteristics**:
- **Jargon Level**: Low to Medium - introduces technical terms with context
- **Buzzword Density**: Minimal - focuses on practical, concrete terms
- **Sensory Language**: Present but not excessive ("clean and simple", "thorough look")
- **Concrete vs. Abstract**: 80/20 ratio - heavily favors concrete examples

## Structural Patterns

### Sentence Style

- **Average length**: 15-20 words
- **Variety**: Medium to High - mixes short punchy sentences with longer explanatory ones
- **Complexity**: Simple to Moderate - avoids convoluted constructions
- **Punctuation**: Standard with occasional informal touches (ellipses "...", parentheses for asides)
- **Rhythm**: Varied - alternates between instructional statements and conversational asides

### Paragraph Style

- **Length**: Short to Medium (1-4 sentences, rarely longer)
- **Organization**: Logical flow with frequent headers and subheaders
- **White space**: Generous - creates visual breathing room
- **Lists**: Frequent use of numbered lists for steps, bullet points for options

### Document Structure Pattern

1. Brief introduction/hook (often with a question or scenario)
2. Context or background information
3. Main content with clear headers
4. Step-by-step instructions (numbered lists)
5. Visual examples (screenshots, images)
6. Downloadable resources or links
7. Encouraging closing statement

## Style Dos and Don'ts

### DO

- Write as if explaining to a colleague over coffee
- Use short paragraphs and generous white space
- Include step-by-step numbered instructions for tutorials
- Provide visual examples (screenshots, diagrams) to support text
- Link to downloadable resources and external references
- Start with a brief context or hook before diving in
- Use direct address ("you") to engage readers
- Explain technical terms when introducing them
- Credit collaborators and reference other experts
- End with encouraging, positive statements
- Use headers and subheaders to organize content
- Share personal experiences and thought processes
- Ask rhetorical questions to engage readers
- Keep sentences clear and concise
- Use active voice predominantly

### DON'T

- Use marketing buzzwords or corporate speak
- Write long, dense paragraphs (break them up)
- Assume readers know technical jargon without explanation
- Be overly formal or academic in tone
- Use passive voice when active is clearer
- Forget to provide practical examples
- Skip the encouraging closing statement
- Omit visual elements when explaining UI/UX concepts
- Make content feel like a lecture
- Use complex sentence structures that obscure meaning
- Forget to acknowledge sources and collaborators
- End on a negative or abrupt note
